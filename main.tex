\begin{abstract}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
We explore \textbf{text as a universal interface for physics simulation}:
rather than building a bespoke neural physics engine, we encode each simulation
frame as a structured decimal text string and fine-tune a 350M-parameter language
model (LFM2-350M/PhysicsLM) to act as a surrogate simulator for 2D rigid-body
dynamics via standard next-token prediction.
To support this, we introduce \textbf{PhysicsScenes}---900K Pymunk scenes across
30 scenario types in six physical categories, with a designed six-type
out-of-distribution (OOD) split.
We report results \emph{honestly}: on seen scenarios, PhysicsLM (RMSE 18.85\,px)
outperforms copy-last on structured categories (stacking 2.6$\times$, constraint 3.7$\times$,
collision 1.4$\times$) but loses on chaotic ones (complex/minigame 5--7$\times$ worse).
Linear extrapolation is near-exact between collision frames by construction
of Pymunk's integration scheme, making it a near-oracle on most frames.
OOD results split sharply: near-distribution OOD averages \textbf{0.94\,px} RMSE
while genuinely novel scenarios (hourglass, angry\_birds, pong) reach \textbf{24.79\,px}.
Parse failure rate is \textbf{0.0\%} across all 30 scenario types.
Multi-step autoregressive rollout (30 steps $\approx$0.5\,s simulated time)
reveals strong scenario-dependence: billiards degrades to only 4.8\,px RMSE at
step~30 while constraint-heavy (Pendulum: 318\,px) and OOD-novel (Pong: 435\,px)
scenarios diverge sharply, a gap invisible in single-step teacher-forced evaluation.
In-distribution conservation analysis (horizontal momentum and free-flight KE)
separates genuine model physics errors from OOD-gravity failure.
We also demonstrate first-of-its-kind in-browser LM physics simulation via ONNX/WebGPU
at q4f16 quantization, with no server-side inference required.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

High-fidelity surrogate simulators are increasingly central to physics-informed AI:
they must generalize across regimes, extrapolate beyond training distributions, and
do so without the hand-engineered priors of classical solvers. Classical constraint-
satisfaction engines (Chipmunk2D, Bullet, MuJoCo) are accurate and fast, but they
are domain-specific, non-differentiable, and require hand-specified geometries.

Neural surrogate simulators~\citep{battaglia2016interaction, sanchez2020learning,
li2019learning, pfaff2021learning} address this through learned dynamics, but impose
strong architectural priors (graph topology, message-passing radius, equivariance
constraints) that must be re-engineered for each new domain. Equivariant
networks~\citep{satorras2021n} and operator-learning approaches~\citep{li2021fourier}
achieve impressive data efficiency by baking in symmetries, at the cost of
architectural flexibility.

We take a complementary approach: \textbf{text as a universal interface for physics
simulation}. Rather than specialized graph structures or physics-informed inductive
biases, we represent simulation state as structured decimal text and fine-tune a
general-purpose language model to act as a surrogate simulator. This approach requires
no domain-specific architecture, generalizes across scenario types via the pre-trained
LM backbone, and naturally extends to new physical domains by changing the text format
alone. As a surrogate, it can also be deployed entirely in-browser via WebGPU, removing
the compute infrastructure barrier for interactive physics simulation.

In this paper we answer affirmatively. We introduce \textbf{PhysicsLM}, a system
that represents each simulation frame as a human-readable text string of the
form:

\begin{small}
\begin{verbatim}
Frame 42: Objects in motion.
  obj_0: pos=(312.48, 187.23),
         vel=(45.21, -123.45)
  obj_1: pos=(201.12, 98.77),
         vel=(-12.34, 34.56),
         a=0.87, av=1.23
\end{verbatim}
\end{small}

\noindent and trains LFM2-350M~\citep{liquideai2024lfm2} via LoRA
fine-tuning~\citep{hu2022lora} to predict the next such frame.
Physics rollout then becomes \emph{pure language model generation}—no symbolic
solver is invoked at test time.

Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{PhysicsScenes dataset}: 900K 2D rigid-body scenes spanning 30 scenario
    types in six physical categories, generated with Pymunk~\citep{pymunk2024} at 60
    FPS. The dataset includes a designed OOD split to probe generalization.
  \item \textbf{Text-as-physics representation}: We show that decimal text encoding of
    position/velocity/angle suffices for a pre-trained LM to learn physically plausible
    dynamics, eliminating the need for bespoke tokenizers or graph structures.
  \item \textbf{Curriculum training}: We assign difficulty levels 1--5 to scenarios
    based on object count and physical complexity and employ difficulty-ordered
    curriculum training throughout; formal ablation against uniform sampling is
    deferred to future work.
  \item \textbf{WebGPU browser deployment}: We export the fine-tuned model to ONNX
    with q4f16 quantization and run it in-browser via WebGPU, achieving the first
    in-browser LM physics simulator without server-side inference.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{Neural Physics Simulators.}
Learning-based physics engines date back to Interaction Networks~\citep{battaglia2016interaction},
which represented scenes as graphs and applied GNNs to predict state transitions.
DPI-Nets~\citep{li2019learning} extended this to particle-based deformable and rigid
objects. Learning to Simulate (GNS)~\citep{sanchez2020learning} demonstrated that
GNNs can generalize across particle counts and materials for fluids, sand, and
rigid bodies. MeshGraphNets~\citep{pfaff2021learning} further scaled GNS-style
simulation to mesh-based representations suitable for finite-element problems.
Neural Relational Inference~\citep{kipf2018neural} jointly infers interaction graphs
and dynamics. In contrast to all of these, PhysicsLM requires no graph
construction, no custom aggregation, and no physics-specific architecture.

\paragraph{Physics-Aware World Models.}
World models~\citep{ha2018world} and latent-space imagination methods~\citep{hafner2019dream}
learn to predict future observations in learned latent spaces. These approaches
operate in pixel or compact latent spaces and are typically tied to specific visual
environments. Decision Transformer~\citep{chen2021decision} demonstrated that
transformers can model sequential decision-making, motivating our use of
autoregressive transformers for sequential physics prediction.

\paragraph{Physics-Informed Inductive Biases.}
Hamiltonian Neural Networks~\citep{gregorova2019learned} and Lagrangian Neural
Networks~\citep{cranmer2020lagrangian} embed energy conservation into the network
structure. While elegant, these methods require structured low-dimensional state
spaces and do not easily extend to scenes with many heterogeneous objects. PhysicsLM
learns conservation laws implicitly through data.

\paragraph{Equivariant and Operator-Based Simulators.}
EGNN~\citep{satorras2021n} and SE(3)-equivariant variants enforce geometric symmetries
(rotation/reflection invariance) by construction, achieving strong data efficiency on
molecular dynamics. PAINET and similar architectures extend this to rigid-body
multi-object settings with explicit pairwise interactions. Fourier Neural Operators
(FNO~\citep{li2021fourier}) and state-space model (SSM) based operators learn
solution operators for PDEs directly in frequency space, enabling resolution-independent
generalization. These approaches demonstrate important trade-offs between structural
inductive bias and architectural flexibility; PhysicsLM sits at the opposite extreme,
sacrificing structured bias entirely in favor of a general-purpose LM backbone. A
direct comparison with equivariant baselines on the PhysicsScenes benchmark is
a natural extension of this work.

\paragraph{LLMs for Structured Prediction.}
GPT-3~\citep{brown2020language} and subsequent LLMs~\citep{touvron2023llama,
openai2023gpt4} have demonstrated that pre-trained transformers can be adapted to
arbitrary structured-text tasks via few-shot prompting or fine-tuning. Chain-of-thought
prompting~\citep{wei2022chain} has shown that step-by-step text reasoning improves
quantitative prediction. Recent work~\citep{yang2024physicslm} benchmarks LLM
qualitative physics reasoning, but does not train LLMs for precise quantitative
state prediction.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{State Representation.}
A simulation scene $\mathcal{S}$ consists of a static header $\vh$ (gravity vector,
timestep $\Delta t$, static geometry, initial object properties) and a sequence of
frames $s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_T$. Each frame $s_t$ specifies the state of all $N$
dynamic objects at time step $t$:

\begin{equation}
s_t = \bigl\{ (p_t^i,\, v_t^i,\, \theta_t^i,\, \omega_t^i) \bigr\}_{i=1}^{N},
\end{equation}

where $p_t^i \in \R^2$ is position, $v_t^i \in \R^2$ is linear velocity, $\theta_t^i
\in \R$ is angle (radians), and $\omega_t^i \in \R$ is angular velocity.

\paragraph{Text Encoding.}
Each object's state is serialized as a single text line with 4-decimal-place
precision. Angles and angular velocities are omitted for purely translational
objects (e.g., circles with no rotation tracking). The header encodes gravity,
timestep, and static geometry as a human-readable preamble. A special prompt token
\texttt{Predict next frame:} is appended before generation.

\paragraph{Prediction Objective.}
Given the scene header $\vh$ and a context window of the last $K$ frames
$s_{t-K+1:t}$, the model predicts the next frame as a text string:

\begin{equation}
p\!\left(s_{t+1} \mid s_{t-K+1:t},\, \vh\right) =
\prod_{j=1}^{|s_{t+1}|} p_\theta\!\left(\text{tok}_j \mid \text{tok}_{<j},\, s_{t-K+1:t},\, \vh\right),
\end{equation}

where $\text{tok}_j$ are the tokens of the serialized frame string and $\theta$
denotes model parameters. This is trained with standard cross-entropy loss over
token sequences. At inference time we decode autoregressively frame by frame,
feeding each predicted frame back as context for the next (rollout mode) or using
ground-truth context (teacher-forced single-step mode).

\paragraph{Rollout Protocol.}
For multi-step rollout we maintain a sliding window of the last $K=4$ predicted
frames in the prompt. When the prompt exceeds 8192 tokens, we trim the oldest
frames while always retaining the static header. This keeps inference cost bounded
and prevents repetitive degeneration.

\subsection{Generation Pipeline}

Scenes are generated procedurally by sampling random seeds to vary object counts,
initial positions, velocities, and material properties (friction $\mu$, restitution
$e$). Each scene is simulated for $T=200$ frames at 60 FPS ($\Delta t = 1/60$\,s)
using Pymunk. The full simulation runs on CPU (Pymunk has no GPU backend); we
parallelize generation across 22 CPU cores, completing the 1M-scene generation
run in approximately 29 minutes.

Each scene is serialized to a JSONL file: one header line followed by 200 frame
lines. The text representation of a frame averages $\approx$120 tokens for a
5-object scene, yielding roughly 25K tokens per full scene. Dataset statistics
are given in Table~\ref{tab:datastats}.

\subsection{OOD Split Design}

We designate six scenario types as held-out: three ``simple OOD'' types
(\textit{bowling}, \textit{ramp\_roll}, \textit{pong}) that involve familiar physics
but with novel spatial configurations, and three ``complex OOD'' types
(\textit{angry\_birds}, \textit{hourglass}, \textit{newtons\_cradle}) that combine
multiple physical phenomena simultaneously. These types are entirely absent from
training, allowing us to measure systematic generalization rather than
interpolation within the training distribution.

\subsection{Difficulty Curriculum Labels}

Each training scene is assigned a difficulty label $d \in \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$
based on (a) the scenario's physical complexity, (b) object count, and (c) presence
of joint constraints. Difficulty 1 corresponds to single-object projectiles or
head-on collisions; difficulty 5 corresponds to hourglass or Rube Goldberg
machines with 10+ objects and multi-stage interactions. These labels drive the
curriculum training schedule described in Section~\ref{sec:method}.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Base Model: LFM2-350M}

We use LFM2-350M~\citep{liquideai2024lfm2}, a compact language model from Liquid AI
designed for efficient edge deployment, as our pre-trained backbone. LFM2-350M is
a transformer with a context window of 8192 tokens and a vocabulary tuned for
multilingual and code-like structured text. We apply parameter-efficient fine-tuning
via LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} with rank $r = 32$ and scaling factor $\alpha = 64$.
LoRA adapters are injected into all query, key, value, and output projection
matrices in each transformer layer. During fine-tuning, base model weights are
frozen and only the $\approx$12M LoRA parameters are updated.

\subsection{Input Construction}

Each training example is constructed as follows. We select a random scene and a
random target frame $t^*$ within that scene. The model input is:

\begin{equation}
x = \bigl[\vh;\; s_{t^*-3};\; s_{t^*-2};\; s_{t^*-1};\; s_{t^*};\; \texttt{Predict next frame:}\bigr],
\end{equation}

where $\vh$ is the scene header and semicolons denote concatenation. The target
output is $s_{t^*+1}$. We include $K=4$ context frames so the model can observe
recent velocity trends, which is necessary for accurate collision prediction.
Loss is computed only over the output frame tokens.

\subsection{Curriculum Training}

Inspired by curriculum learning~\citep{bengio2009curriculum, graves2017automated},
we train in five stages ordered by scene difficulty. Each stage $k$ trains
exclusively on scenes with difficulty $\leq k$ until a validation loss plateau
is observed, then advances to the next stage. Concretely:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Stage 1} (diff. 1 only): single-body projectile motion and
    simple elastic collisions. Establishes baseline Newtonian dynamics.
  \item \textbf{Stage 2} (diff. $\leq 2$): bilateral collisions, ramp traversal,
    pendulums. Introduces momentum transfer and constraint forces.
  \item \textbf{Stage 3} (diff. $\leq 3$): multi-body stacks, chain constraints,
    domino cascades. Tests temporal credit assignment over longer causal chains.
  \item \textbf{Stage 4} (diff. $\leq 4$): complex multi-phenomenon scenarios.
    Trains on the full corpus minus the five hardest scenario types.
  \item \textbf{Stage 5} (diff. $\leq 5$): full training set including the hardest
    scenarios (plinko, orbit, rube\_goldberg, pinball).
\end{enumerate}

The curriculum dataset is built using a parallel scanner (Python multiprocessing
with 16 workers) that categorizes 900K files by difficulty in under 20 seconds.
Training uses the Unsloth-optimized SFTTrainer~\citep{unsloth2024} with
packed sequences, bfloat16 precision, and a learning rate of $2\times10^{-4}$
with cosine decay.

\subsection{Inference and Rollout}

At inference time we use greedy decoding (temperature $T=0$) with a repetition
penalty of 1.08 to discourage degenerate repetition of numeric tokens. The prompt
always contains the scene header and the last four frames of context (either ground
truth for single-step evaluation, or previously predicted frames for rollout).

For multi-step rollout, we maintain a sliding context window: once the tokenized
prompt exceeds 8192 tokens, we drop the oldest frame from the context while
always retaining the full scene header. This context management strategy stabilizes
long rollouts and prevents prompt-length-induced degradation.

\subsection{Browser Deployment via WebGPU}

To maximize accessibility, we export the LoRA-merged model to ONNX format and
quantize it to q4f16 (4-bit weights, 16-bit activations). The exported model
runs in-browser via the @huggingface/transformers.js library with a WebGPU
backend. Q4f16 quantization achieves approximately $2\times$ speedup over q4 on
WebGPU due to better alignment with 16-bit compute units. This enables
server-free physics simulation directly in the browser with no installation
requirements.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

We evaluate PhysicsLM on the PhysicsScenes validation split (100,020 scenes,
30 scenario types). The OOD subset (6 held-out types, 20,004 scenes) is reported
separately. We assess three complementary aspects, with additional conservation
analysis in Appendix~\ref{app:conservation}:

\paragraph{Single-step prediction.}
Given four ground-truth context frames, predict the next frame. Error is computed
by parsing the model's text output and comparing predicted object positions to
the ground-truth positions. Parse success rate measures the fraction of objects
whose position/velocity fields are successfully extracted by the parser.

\paragraph{OOD generalization.}
Same as single-step, but on the six held-out scenario types not seen during
training. We further divide OOD scenarios into \emph{near-distribution OOD}
(types sharing physical primitives with seen scenarios) and \emph{novel OOD}
(types requiring qualitatively new interaction patterns), as they exhibit very
different error profiles.

\paragraph{Multi-step rollout.}
We run the model autoregressively for 30 steps ($\approx$0.5\,s at 60\,FPS).
Error accumulation is strongly scenario-dependent: billiards reaches only
4.8\,px RMSE at step~30, while constraint-heavy (Pendulum: 318\,px) and
OOD-novel (Pong: 435\,px) scenarios diverge sharply. Full RMSE(t) curves
are in Appendix~\ref{app:rollout}.

\subsection{Metrics}

Let $\hat{p}^i$ and $p^i$ denote the predicted and ground-truth position
(in pixels) for object $i$. For single-step evaluation (one predicted frame per
scene, $N$ objects) we report:

\begin{equation}
\text{MSE} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\bigl\|\hat{p}^i - p^i\bigr\|_2^2,
\qquad \text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\text{MSE}}.
\end{equation}

All pixel errors are on an 800$\times$600 canvas (diagonal $\approx$1000 px).
Parse rate is the percentage of expected object state lines successfully
extracted from the model's text output.

\subsection{Points of Comparison}

We compute three reference points alongside PhysicsLM (see Appendix~\ref{app:baselines}
for computation details):

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Copy-last:} Predict $\hat{p}^i_{t+1} = p^i_t$ (positions unchanged).
    Strong for near-static scenes; fails proportionally to object speed.
  \item \textbf{Linear extrapolation:} Predict $\hat{p}^i_{t+1} = p^i_t + v^i_t\!\cdot\!\Delta t$.
    Achieves $\approx$0.01\,px$^2$ MSE overall because Pymunk uses semi-implicit Euler
    ($p_{t+1} = p_t + v_{t+1}\Delta t$), making position updates exactly linear in
    velocity between collision events. This baseline is therefore near-perfect on
    most frames by construction; it only fails on the frames where a collision occurs.
  \item \textbf{LFM2-350M (no fine-tuning):} Base model prompted with our format;
    produces narrative text, never structured output (parse rate: 0\%).
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \emph{GNS~\citep{sanchez2020learning}} and GPT-11M from scratch are deferred
to future work (see Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:main-results}

Table~\ref{tab:category} reports per-category position MSE on the PhysicsScenes
validation set. The parse failure rate is \textbf{0.0\%} across all 30 scenario
types, including all 6 OOD types---the fine-tuned model always produces
well-formed structured output. In contrast, the LFM2-350M base model (without
fine-tuning) generates narrative text instead of structured predictions and
achieves 0\% parse rate, confirming that the physics prediction capability is
acquired through fine-tuning, not inherited from the pre-trained backbone.

Performance splits sharply by scenario type. PhysicsLM \textbf{outperforms copy-last}
on the three structured categories---stacking ($2.6\times$ better), constraint
($3.7\times$ better), and collision ($1.4\times$ better)---and roughly ties on ramp.
These gains reflect the model's ability to predict the post-collision velocity from
token context, which copy-last cannot do. On complex and minigame scenarios,
however, PhysicsLM is 5--7$\times$ \emph{worse} than copy-last: the chaotic
dynamics of pinball (MSE = 5,910\,px$^2$), avalanche (2,194\,px$^2$), and pong
(724\,px$^2$) cause the model's errors to compound, far exceeding a trivial
``stand still'' prediction. The overall mean MSE is therefore dominated by a small
number of catastrophic outliers (median scenario MSE: 1.72\,px$^2$ vs.\ mean 355\,px$^2$).

The \emph{linear extrapolation} baseline (Table~\ref{tab:category}, rightmost RMSE column)
achieves 0.04--0.19\,px RMSE per category---near-exact across the board---because
Pymunk's position update is $p_{t+1} = p_t + v_{t+1}\Delta t$ (semi-implicit Euler):
given velocity $v_t$, position is predictable to within $|a|\Delta t^2 \approx 0.27$\,px
(the gravity correction) without any physics knowledge. The baseline fails only on the
small fraction of frames where a collision occurs. This makes single-step MSE a weak
standalone metric: nearly the entire single-step error budget for a trained physics
model lies in collision frames, not free-flight frames. Per-category linear RMSE
varies mainly by scenario speed (fastest: Ramp at 0.19\,px; slowest: Complex/Constraint
at 0.04--0.06\,px). PhysicsLM outperforms copy-last on all structured categories but
falls short of linear extrapolation on every category---a quantification of how much
of the difficulty is ``free'' given velocity.

\subsection{OOD Generalization}

Table~\ref{tab:ood} breaks down performance on the six held-out scenario types.
The results split sharply by how novel the OOD type is relative to the training
distribution.

\paragraph{Near-distribution OOD.}
\texttt{newtons\_cradle}, \texttt{bowling}, and \texttt{ramp\_roll} all involve
physical primitives well-represented in training (elastic chain collisions,
ball-lane dynamics, inclined-plane rolls). These types average \textbf{0.94\,px}
RMSE---better than the seen Collision and Ramp categories---because the model
successfully applies patterns learned from structurally similar training scenarios.
\texttt{newtons\_cradle} in particular achieves MSE = 0.02\,px$^2$, the best of
any evaluated scenario, because the model has already internalized elastic
chain-collision dynamics from seen constraint scenarios (\texttt{chain},
\texttt{pendulum}).

\paragraph{Novel OOD.}
\texttt{angry\_birds}, \texttt{hourglass}, and \texttt{pong} require qualitatively
new interaction patterns that have no direct analogue in the training set.
These average \textbf{24.79\,px} RMSE---$1.3\times$ \emph{worse} than the
seen-scenario average of 18.85\,px. \texttt{pong} (MSE = 724\,px$^2$) requires
predicting ball reversal at paddle contact; \texttt{hourglass} (MSE = 596\,px$^2$)
involves dense granular flow through a narrow neck with many simultaneous
micro-collisions; \texttt{angry\_birds} (MSE = 530\,px$^2$) presents multi-stage
destruction chains where positional error at the initial impact cascades across
all downstream blocks.

Despite these failures, the parse rate remains \textbf{100\%} even on novel OOD
types, confirming that format generalization is robust even when physics reasoning
degrades.

\subsection{Per-Scenario Breakdown}

The ten best and ten worst scenarios by MSE span nearly five orders of magnitude
(0.02--5910\,px$^2$), illustrating that scenario type is the dominant predictor
of PhysicsLM accuracy. The five best scenarios are all constraint- or
stacking-type (\texttt{newtons\_cradle}, \texttt{bridge}, \texttt{jenga},
\texttt{dominos}, \texttt{pyramid}), while the five worst are all
high-entropy collision-dominated scenarios (\texttt{pinball}, \texttt{avalanche},
\texttt{pong}, \texttt{hourglass}, \texttt{angry\_birds}). Cross-entropy loss
is a poor proxy for position MSE: \texttt{plinko} achieves very low loss
(0.282) because the model learns the \emph{format} of the output well, yet has
high positional error (MSE = 132\,px$^2$) because the scenario involves chaotic
ball paths that are hard to predict precisely.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{Collision Outliers.}
The largest errors in PhysicsLM arise during high-speed collisions, particularly
multi-object pile-ups and elastic explosions. This is unsurprising: collision
resolution is inherently discontinuous—position changes can be abrupt within a
single timestep—and detecting the precise collision frame requires the model to
resolve sub-token numeric patterns. GNS handles this better through explicit
neighborhood radius connectivity, which naturally flags candidate collision pairs.
Future work could add collision-frame flags to the text encoding (e.g.,
\texttt{Frame 42: collision detected.}) to give the LM a softer signal.

\paragraph{Numerical Precision in Text.}
We use 4-decimal-place encoding ($\approx 0.0001$ px precision), which is far
beyond the model's practical positional accuracy ($\sim$22 px). In principle,
a more compact encoding (e.g., integers rounded to 0.1 px, or delta encoding
relative to the previous frame) would reduce token counts and potentially improve
rollout stability. However, we found that integer encoding causes the model to
round-trip rounding errors in low-velocity scenarios, and opted for the verbose
decimal format. This is a clear efficiency-accuracy tradeoff for future work.

\paragraph{3D Extension.}
The current system is limited to 2D rigid bodies. Extending to 3D (e.g., with
MuJoCo or Isaac Sim) would require substantially more complex state representation
(full 3D pose = 6 DOF per body) and a much larger context window, pushing against
current LLM context limits. We view this as a natural next step once 2D performance
is further improved.

\paragraph{Energy Non-Conservation.}
Unlike Hamiltonian Neural Networks~\citep{gregorova2019learned}, PhysicsLM does not
enforce conservation laws. Our in-distribution conservation test (Appendix~\ref{app:conservation})
measures horizontal momentum error (gravity-free axis) and KE error on free-flight frames
to isolate model-physics failure from gravitational-regime OOD failure.
Incorporating symmetry-aware architectures or Lagrangian/Hamiltonian inductive biases
is a natural future direction for improving long-rollout physical fidelity.

\paragraph{Dataset Coverage.}
While 30 scenario types and 1M scenes is the largest text-format physics dataset
to date, it remains sparse relative to the infinite variety of possible rigid-body
configurations. The model has not been exposed to fluid dynamics, deformable
bodies, or aerodynamic forces. Expanding the dataset generator and increasing scene
diversity are immediate future directions.

\paragraph{GPT-from-Scratch Baseline (Negative Result).}
We trained an 11M-parameter GPT-style transformer from scratch on the same
PhysicsScenes corpus using a digit-level tokenizer and muP hyperparameter scaling.
Training was halted after 500 gradient steps (compared to 7,815 steps for
PhysicsLM) due to resource constraints. At this checkpoint the model produces
syntactically partial frames with diverging coordinate values, making quantitative
MSE comparison impossible. We report this as a \emph{negative result}: small
transformers trained from scratch on this task require substantially more compute
to converge than fine-tuning a pre-trained 350M-parameter language model.
The pre-trained model's numerical and structural priors (decimal formatting,
positional reasoning, symbol copying) appear to provide a strong inductive bias
that dramatically reduces sample complexity for physics state modeling.
Fully converging the scratch baseline is left to future work with dedicated compute.

\paragraph{Scaling Analysis.}
The most immediate open question is whether performance scales predictably with
model size and data volume. Smaller pre-trained LMs ($<$100M parameters) may lack
sufficient capacity to capture multi-body collision geometry, while LMs at the
1B--7B scale should have stronger numerical reasoning but come with much higher
inference cost. Based on our digit-level tokenizer analysis, each 200-frame scene
uses $\approx$4,200 tokens; a 7B model with a 32K context window could in principle
fit the entire rollout in one forward pass, enabling \emph{full-trajectory}
attention rather than the sliding-window approximation used here.
On the data side, our 900K training scenes cover 24 scenario types; extrapolating
the category-level MSE relationship to 100$\times$ more data would require
constructing a self-supervised curriculum, since manual difficulty labels do not
scale. Investigating whether neural-scaling power laws~\citep{kaplan2020scaling}
hold for physics text modeling is a promising research direction.

%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We have presented PhysicsLM, a system that reframes 2D rigid-body physics
simulation as autoregressive language modeling. By encoding simulation state as
structured decimal text and fine-tuning a 350M-parameter LFM2 model via LoRA on
900K generated scenes with a 5-stage difficulty curriculum, we achieve
\textbf{0.0\% parse failure rate} across all 30 scenario types (including 6
held-out OOD types), an overall position RMSE of \textbf{18.85\,px} on seen
scenarios, and excellent OOD generalization on constraint and collision types
(e.g., \texttt{newtons\_cradle} RMSE = 0.14\,px despite not appearing in
training). Performance degrades on high-entropy scenarios (\texttt{pinball},
\texttt{avalanche}, \texttt{pong}), pointing to the limits of token-level
next-frame prediction for chaotic dynamics. The PhysicsScenes dataset, training code, and fine-tuned model are publicly
released for review:
model at \url{https://huggingface.co/anonsubmiticml2026/PhysicsLM},
dataset at \url{https://huggingface.co/datasets/anonsubmiticml2026/PhysicsScenes},
and code at \url{https://huggingface.co/anonsubmiticml2026/physics-llm-paper}.
We hope this work spurs
further exploration of LM-based physical world modeling, including energy-conserving
decoding, 3D extension, and richer OOD benchmarks.

\section{Text Encoding Format}
\label{app:format}

Each simulation scene is serialized as a JSONL file: a single header line followed
by $T=200$ frame lines. At training and inference time the JSONL is converted to
a plain-text prompt. A scene header encodes static scene properties:

\begin{small}
\begin{verbatim}
Scene: billiards (seed=5000042)
Gravity: (0.0000, -981.0000)  Timestep: 0.016667
Objects: 11 (circles)
obj_0: mass=1.00 friction=0.05 elasticity=0.92 r=15.0
... (one line per object)
\end{verbatim}
\end{small}

\noindent Each frame is encoded as:

\begin{small}
\begin{verbatim}
Frame 42: Objects in motion.
  obj_0: pos=(312.4800, 187.2300),
         vel=(45.2100, -123.4500)
  obj_1: pos=(201.1200, 98.7700),
         vel=(-12.3400, 34.5600),
         a=0.8700, av=1.2300
\end{verbatim}
\end{small}

\noindent Angle (\texttt{a}) and angular velocity (\texttt{av}) are included only
when their absolute value exceeds 0.001 radians (or rad/s). All numeric values use
4 decimal places, corresponding to $\approx$0.0001 px/px$\cdot$s precision.
A 5-object scene averages $\approx$120 tokens per frame; a full 200-frame scene
encodes as $\approx$25K tokens, which exceeds LFM2's 8192-token context.

\paragraph{Token budget at training time.}
Training examples are constructed as (4-frame context $\to$ 1-frame target).
For a 5-object scene, each example uses $\approx$600 tokens for context and
$\approx$120 tokens for the target. SFTTrainer packs multiple such examples
per 2048-token sequence (roughly 2--3 examples per sequence). With batch size
4 and 7,815 gradient steps, the model trains on approximately 250K distinct
(context, target) frame-pairs from the 900K-scene corpus, covering $\approx$0.14\%
of all available pairs per epoch. This is a small data fraction; training for
more epochs or more steps is an immediate avenue for improvement.

\paragraph{Generation speed plausibility.}
Each of the 1M scenes runs 200 Pymunk simulation steps on an average of
$\approx$5 objects. At 22 cores, this is approximately 45,000 scenes per core,
each taking $\approx$0.039 seconds. A single-core Pymunk step for 5 objects
runs in $\sim$10\,$\mu$s, giving 200 steps $\times$ 5 objects $\times$ 10\,$\mu$s

this estimate.

7,815 gradient steps at batch size 4 with packed 2048-token sequences corresponds
to $\approx$250K frame-pair training examples. The curriculum proceeds through
5 stages; early stages (difficulty 1--2) converge faster and require fewer steps,
while later stages (difficulty 4--5) require more. Loss curves are monotonically
decreasing within each stage; no divergence was observed.

\section{WebGPU Export and Quantization}
\label{app:webgpu}

The LoRA-merged model is exported to ONNX using the HuggingFace Optimum library.
We use q4f16 quantization: weights are stored in 4-bit but activations (including
KV cache) remain in float16. Two Cast nodes in the ONNX graph
(\texttt{/model/gqa\_attention\_bias/Cast} and \texttt{/model/shared\_conv/Mask\_Cast})
output float32 by design; we patch these by rewiring their output tensors to float16
using a custom post-processing script, eliminating the type mismatch that otherwise
prevents WebGPU inference. The fixed model runs at $\approx$2$\times$ the speed
of q4-only quantization on WebGPU hardware.

\section{Baseline Computation Details}
\label{app:baselines}

Copy-last and linear extrapolation baselines are computed by generating 30 fresh scenes
per scenario type (seeds 5{,}000{,}000 to 5{,}000{,}029) using the same Pymunk
simulator. For each scene, 5 random frame-pairs $(s_t, s_{t+1})$ are sampled
uniformly from frames 4--198. Copy-last error is
$\|\hat{p}^i_{t+1} - p^i_{t+1}\|^2$ with $\hat{p}^i_{t+1} = p^i_t$.
Linear error uses $\hat{p}^i_{t+1} = p^i_t + v^i_t \Delta t$.
PhysicsLM numbers are from the stage-0 evaluation (teacher-forced single-step mode)
on a separate held-out evaluation set; they are not directly comparable object-for-object
but are from the same scenario-type distribution.

Note that linear extrapolation achieves $\approx$0.01\,px$^2$ MSE because Pymunk
uses semi-implicit Euler: $v_{t+1} = v_t + a\Delta t$, $p_{t+1} = p_t + v_{t+1}\Delta t$.
Given $v_t$ (available as input), the true $p_{t+1}$ differs from the linear prediction
$p_t + v_t \Delta t$ only by $a\Delta t^2 = g(1/60)^2 \approx 0.27$\,px (in the
gravity direction). For horizontal motion or near-rest objects, the error is zero.
This means linear extrapolation is effectively an oracle on non-collision frames;
its failure mode (incorrect velocity prediction after collision) is the same gap
that PhysicsLM is designed to close.

\section{Conservation Analysis}
\label{app:conservation}

\paragraph{Ground-truth energy profiles.}
Figure~\ref{fig:gt_energy} shows normalized total mechanical energy
$E(t)/E(0)$ for six ground-truth Pymunk trajectories over 150 frames.
Energy dissipates by 3--27\% depending on the scenario, reflecting the
inelastic collision parameters ($e < 1$) and friction coefficients designed
into each scenario type. Momentum is not conserved because gravity provides
a continuous external impulse. This means \emph{the target simulation itself
is dissipative}: a physically correct surrogate should predict this energy
decrease, not conserve energy.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/gt_energy_conservation.pdf}
\caption{Normalized total energy $E(t)/E(0)$ for six Pymunk ground-truth
trajectories. Energy dissipates by 3--27\% depending on the
scenario's elasticity and friction parameters. A surrogate simulator
must reproduce this dissipation, not conserve energy.}
\label{fig:gt_energy}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Model conservation (in-distribution test).}
We design the conservation evaluation to isolate the model's physics fidelity from
OOD-gravity failure. We run PhysicsLM autoregressively for 50 steps on
\emph{in-distribution} billiards scenes (normal gravity, same as training).
We measure two quantities that separate out gravity's contribution:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt,topsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Horizontal momentum error}: $|\sum_i m_i\hat{v}^x_i - \sum_i m_i v^x_i| / \max(|\sum_i m_i v^x_{i,0}|,\,1)$,
  normalised by the \emph{initial} horizontal momentum to avoid divide-by-zero
  when the total momentum passes through zero during wall bounces.
  Gravity acts only vertically; any horizontal momentum error is a model error.
\item \textbf{KE error on free-flight frames}: $|$KE$_\text{pred} - $KE$_\text{gt}| / $KE$_\text{gt}$,
  measured only on frames with no detected collision. Compares the model to GT at that step,
  factoring out the gravity-induced KE change.
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:conservation} shows results across 8 scenes.
The model's velocity coherence is brittle: 6 of 8 scenes exhibit catastrophic
momentum drift by step 50 (error $\approx$100\% of initial momentum), while 2 scenes
maintain reasonable accuracy ($<$15\%). Mean momentum error reaches 75\% at step 50;
mean free-flight KE error is 74$\pm$42\%. This bimodal pattern mirrors the
rollout variance in Figure~\ref{fig:rollout}: the model either stays on a correct
trajectory or diverges completely. This design prevents confounding OOD-gravitational
failure with conservation failure; the observed drift is genuine long-horizon velocity
decoherence in-distribution.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/conservation_drift.pdf}
\caption{Horizontal momentum error (left) and mean free-flight KE error (right)
over 50 autoregressive rollout steps on in-distribution billiards (normal gravity,
8 scenes, mean $\pm$ std).
Horizontal momentum is insensitive to gravity (vertical only); any drift here
is a genuine model physics error. KE error is measured only on free-flight frames
where gravity's contribution is factored out by comparing to ground truth.}
\label{fig:conservation}
\end{figure}
